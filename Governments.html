Governmental Actions

United States

Within the sphere of the western world, the United States stands as a pillar of influence on policy making and globally impactful programmes. This makes its approach to global clean water and sanitation particularly significant.

In 2021, the Bi-partisan Infrastructure Law earmarked $48.4 billion for modernizing and maintaining existing water infrastructure, with a strong focus on removing lead pipes and increasing nationwide water quality for citizens. At the same time, under the current administration there has been a rollback of some environmental protections and a narrowing of what can be protected under the Clean Water Act (CWA).

Internationally, the US record is mixed. In 2010 the US abstained from voting on a UN resolution recognising access to clean water and sanitation as a human right, alongside 40 other countries. Its objections focused on the wording and legal implications of the resolution, rather than rejecting the importance of clean water itself.

Global progress

Beyond the US, many countries are making tangible progress towards the goal of universal access to clean water and sanitation. In the UK, the Blackburn Meadows wastewater treatment works offers one example. First commissioned in 2022 and completed in 2025, it can process around 4,000 litres of wastewater per second, with greater efficacy at removing contaminants than previous plants. It was also constructed with a highly renewable supply chain, resulting in a relatively small carbon footprint.

In Kenya, the Kenya Towns Sustainable Water Supply project has focused on the logistics of distributing clean water across towns and cities. While treatment facilities and reservoirs exist, infrastructure to move water to where it is needed has often lagged behind. By building new sewer pipes and dams and extending existing supply lines, the project aims to keep pace with the demands of a rapidly growing population.

Looking ahead

Overall, countries are still making important strides towards achieving Sustainable Development Goal 6 on clean water and sanitation. However, environmental and water standards are increasingly used as political tools, with policies changing according to the priorities of current governments rather than a consistent view of clean water as a human right. Even so, progress compared to five or ten years ago remains clear, with new infrastructure, technologies, and legal frameworks continuing to emerge around the world.
