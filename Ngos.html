NGOs

Charities and clean water

Charities have the freedom to cater to a specific need or cause as opposed to corporations who focus on the consumer, and governments who must undertake a myriad of issues. So, is this freedom effective in helping to promote and encourage clean water and sanitation over the globe?

WaterAid and advocacy

One of the household names in this space is WaterAid. Founded in 1981 in response to the UN international drinking water decade, they started with projects in Zambia and Sri Lanka, expanding to 30 countries today. With a variety of core aims including education, sanitation, hygiene and more, aiming to highlight the key points undermining the current systems.

Given that charities such as water aid do not have a consumer base of sorts, like a corporation, they are more freely able to engage in political lobbying without risk of public backlash. This had led to shifts in policy and public perception, having part in including clean water and sanitation in the UN SDG for 2030.

Challenges and reputational risk

Charities often focus on a wide range of actions to address the underlying issues; this can lead to mismanagement or delays in projects in the long-term if a good structure isn’t present. This can then in turn be damaging to the public perception which will harm funding initiatives, circling back round to delays in projects. As they have the freedom to cater to specific needs, there will be a high expectation placed upon them to succeed.

With the growing expansion into the digital world this problem is enhanced, for any public facing entity or persons. Any mistakes broadcasted online can immediately sway public opinion before an actual narrative can be established. An example of this being a sexual assault scandal within Water Aid in late 2010s. This called the entire charity into question and people began to doubt the veracity of its claims about a safe workplace environment, spilling over into questions about its charitable work. Especially as the LHWP (Lesotho highlands water project) corruption scandal was still fresh in people’s minds.

Looking ahead

Charities are both more free and more restricted than other public entities. Less beholden to shareholders or public interest groups, but rather to the world at large and everyone’s judgement. Despite this, massive successes have been achieved over the decades by various organizations. Showing that a single focus on a large issue, while recognizing it has many parts, can be effective.
