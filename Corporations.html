Corporate Actions

Corporate views on water and sustainability

In the corporate space there is often a myriad of conflicting views towards wider humanitarian efforts. These range from paying lip service to concerns, to actively running charitable endeavours alongside the day-to-day use of the company’s products by consumers.

AI and data centres

Hitting the headlines in recent years has been the explosion of AI. Initially used only for highly specific tasks, it is now present in everyday life—from search summaries online to being integrated into products themselves as a marketing point. So what are the larger impacts of this growing sector in the tech industry?

An oft-cited statistic is that each prompt for a large language model (LLM), such as ChatGPT or Gemini, uses as much as a glass of water (or half a litre) to cool the data centres. However, this is not necessarily the case. A study by the University of California found that each query uses approximately 14 ml of water, though this can vary depending on model and prompt size. From industry leaders and company representatives, these concerns are sometimes outright dismissed or labelled as fake—with the CEO of OpenAI (the company behind ChatGPT) addressing them as “totally insane” and noting that “humans also take energy to train up,” as recently as February 2026.

Companies beyond tech

Companies outside the tech sphere show a much wider range of views and actions depending on the corporate niche they occupy. A report from the Valuing Water Finance Initiative Benchmark paints a more positive picture: that companies are gradually moving towards more sustainable initiatives and increasing safeguards and water quality standards within their own supply chains. But the question raised is whether these actions are enough. While some corporate oversight exists, few companies fully integrate freshwater sustainability and monitoring into their business plans—more often viewing it as a checklist of standards to meet rather than fully engaging with the goal.

Looking ahead

Overall, there is still much to be done in the corporate sphere. Clean water initiatives need to be more incentivising for companies so they implement recommendations. Companies also need a broader view of long-term planning: doing the bare minimum to protect corporate interests and maintain the current supply chain, without accounting for growth or uncertainty in the wider market, will negatively impact their overall growth. Proposed plans—for example under WASH (Water, Sanitation and Hygiene)—can not only improve water quality and freshwater stability but support corporate growth as well.
